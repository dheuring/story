Honoring Memory: My Motivation for Telling This Story

By Dave Heuring

When I began writing my autobiography about a year ago, one of the first questions I asked myself was why I was born in Lansing, Michigan. My parents had moved there shortly after my father returned from the Korean War. I happened to have his DD-214 discharge document, which listed his unit designation. Out of curiosity, I entered that information into a Google search and was stunned by what I discovered about the so-called "Forgotten War" and, in particular, the harrowing Battle of the Frozen Chosin.

"My father rarely spoke about what he endured during those brutal days. Of the few memories he did share, three stand out: the unbearable cold, the relentless enemy attacks that came in waves throughout the night, and the terrifying moment when he pretended to be dead as enemy soldiers poked him with their rifle barrels after his position was overrun."

Driven to learn more, I conducted extensive research on the battle, even retrieving decades-old documents from the National Archives. What I have uncovered has left me both stunned and filled with a resigned sadness for what my father experienced. At the very least, I have compiled an account of my father, Norman Heuring's, traumatic experience at the Battle of the Frozen Chosin to offer him the respect and gratitude he so deeply deserves posthumously.

As you will read, the battle was horrific and ended with the destruction of the task force to which he belonged. My father served in Company K, 3rd Battalion, 31st Regiment, 7th Infantry Division. His company commander, Captain Robert J. Kitz, survived and later wrote a detailed statement describing the ordeal. His account, as well as many other sources, provided me with a deeper understanding of the unimaginable hardships, acts of bravery, and the true sequence of events that unfolded during those desperate days.

The Hidden Truth

Unfortunately, serious mistakes made by senior officers at the highest levels of command led to the aftermath being shrouded in secrecy: all records were stamped "secret" and sealed, concealing the true story of what happened during those four days and five nights of desperate fighting.

Of the original 3,200 men in this regimental task force, my dad was one of only 385 able-bodied survivors. Yet, he and the others were unjustly labeled as having abandoned their positions and fled. I believe this accusation weighed heavily on him for the rest of his life. It is deeply regrettable that the truth of what this task force endured only emerged many years later, after historians pieced together an accurate account based on interviews with the survivors and declassified documents from the National Archives.

My father passed away in 1998, never knowing that the world would one day recognize the true significance of his task force's sacrifice. The four-day, five-night stand by the task force played a critical role in holding back the Chinese army, preventing them from closing the only escape route to the port at Hungnam. Without their resistance, the Chinese forces could have cut off and destroyed the surrounded Marines and other American units fighting in the Chosin campaign.

"I am therefore dedicating this research and retelling of the Battle of the Frozen Chosin to my dad, Corporal Norman A. Heuring, in honor of his previously unacknowledged but extraordinary courage and sacrifice."

The Origins and Early Course of the Korean War

The Korean War began in the context of post-World War II tensions and the emerging Cold War. After Japan's defeat in 1945, Korea was divided at the 38th parallel: the Soviet Union occupied the north, and the United States the south. This division, intended as a temporary administrative measure, soon became permanent as both sides established rival governments—communist in the North under Kim Il Sung, and anti-communist in the South under Syngman Rhee.

The Invasion: June 25, 1950

In January 1950, U.S. Secretary of State Dean Acheson delivered a speech outlining America's "defensive perimeter" in the Pacific, notably omitting South Korea from the areas the United States would automatically defend. Many historians believe this omission contributed to North Korean leader Kim Il Sung's decision to launch an invasion, under the assumption that the United States would not intervene militarily.

On June 25, 1950, North Korea launched a full-scale invasion of South Korea, aiming to reunify the peninsula by force. North Korean forces, well-equipped with Soviet tanks and artillery, quickly overran South Korean positions, capturing Seoul within days. The United Nations, led by the United States, responded by sending military forces to support South Korea. Despite this intervention, by August, South Korean and UN forces were driven back to a small defensive perimeter around the port city of Pusan, where they made a determined stand against further North Korean advances—an area that became known as the Pusan Perimeter.

The Inchon Landing and Northern Advance

In September 1950, UN forces launched a bold counteroffensive that dramatically shifted the course of the Korean War. Under the command of General Douglas MacArthur, they executed a daring amphibious landing at Inchon, striking far behind North Korean lines. This surprise operation enabled UN troops to break out from the Pusan Perimeter, swiftly recapture Seoul, and force the North Korean army into a rapid retreat.

Following this success, UN and South Korean forces advanced northward, crossing the 38th parallel and pushing deep into North Korea, reaching the Yalu River near the Chinese border by late October. During this advance, key units such as the 1st Marine Division and elements of the 7th Infantry Division moved into the rugged terrain around the Chosin Reservoir, setting the stage for one of the war's most intense and challenging battles.

"MacArthur even declared that American troops would be 'home for Christmas.'"

By late October, UN forces approached the Yalu River, Korea's border with China. General Douglas MacArthur and other U.S. leaders, confident in the rapid progress, assured the public that the war would soon be over. Despite warnings from Beijing, the advance continued. In late October and early November, Chinese forces entered the war, launching massive attacks against the advancing UN troops. This intervention caught the UN forces by surprise and quickly changed the momentum of the conflict.

By November 1950, UN troops in northeastern Korea, including those around the Chosin Reservoir, faced a new and far more dangerous enemy in the bitter cold of the Korean winter. It was at this point when my Dad arrived in country and joined his unit, Company K, in a planned attack northward to reach the Chinese border.

The Battle of the Chosin Reservoir

Strategic Context and Background

In late November 1950, a conclusion to the Korean War appeared to be close at hand. U.S., Republic of Korea (ROK), and various U.N. units had advanced deep into North Korea in an attempt to destroy any remaining North Korean People's Army (NKPA) units and reunite Korea under one government. Some units had even reached the Yalu River, which separated Korea from Communist China.

On 24 November, the Eighth Army, under the command of Lieutenant General Walton H. Walker, which had been advancing north along the western side of Korea, went on the offensive. On the eastern side, the Army's X Corps was also advancing rapidly towards the Yalu River meeting very little resistance from the NKPA. General Douglas MacArthur, commander of all U.N. forces in Korea, hoped this offensive would finally end the war.

A Catastrophic Intelligence Failure

Yet MacArthur and many on his staff were soon to make one of the worst military intelligence blunders in U.S. Army history. Ignoring reports of contact with Chinese Communist Forces (CCF) troops, MacArthur ordered the Eighth Army and X Corps (combined marine and army divisions) to push on to the Yalu.

But just as U.N. forces launched what was hoped to be the final offensive, hundreds of thousands of Communist Chinese soldiers had already poured into Korea. On the night of 25 November, one day after Eighth Army began its offensive, the CCF struck Eighth Army with massive numbers of troops. Thousands of Chinese soldiers, armed with burp guns and grenades, with bugles blaring, swarmed the American positions. Several American units were overrun and destroyed.

Despite the Chinese Communist Forces' attack on the Eighth Army, X Corps, which included Dad's battalion, went ahead with its planned offensive on November 27. The strategy was for X Corps to push west toward Mupyong-ni, northeast of Kunu-ri, in an effort to sever Chinese supply lines and potentially encircle the enemy forces confronting the Eighth Army. However, General MacArthur had gravely underestimated both the strength and the scale of the Chinese forces facing X Corps—a critical and ultimately disastrous miscalculation.

The Plan

The 1st Marine Division, commanded by Major General O.P. Smith, would spearhead the X Corps attack by advancing up the west side of the Chosin Reservoir. Simultaneously, the 7th Infantry Division—led by Task Force MacLean—would advance along the reservoir's east side, while the 3rd Infantry Division protected the Marines' flanks.

For the 31st Regimental Combat Team (RCT-31) of the 7th Infantry Division, Chinese intervention would prove catastrophic. The unit would be virtually annihilated in the fighting east of the Chosin Reservoir. The rest of this account focuses primarily on RCT-31's ordeal, including the experiences of Company K, 3rd Battalion (3/31)—the unit to which Dad was assigned shortly after his arrival in Korea in mid-November.

Task Force MacLean/Faith: Organization and Deployment

Command Structure

The 31st Regimental Combat Team (RCT-31), known as the Polar Bear Regiment, was a composite force of approximately 3,200 soldiers that included roughly 700 Republic of Korea (ROK) troops integrated within American units. Drawing its primary strength from the 7th Infantry Division, the regiment combined infantry, artillery, armor, and specialized support elements into a cohesive combat formation.

RCT-31, also known as Task Force MacLean, was led by Colonel Allan D. "Mac" MacLean, a 1930 West Point graduate and commander of the 31st Infantry Regiment. MacLean had previously served as a staff officer in the European Theater during World War II and later commanded the 32nd Infantry in Japan.

Lieutenant Colonel Don Faith, commander of the 1st Battalion, 32nd Infantry (1/32st), was considered one of the most promising officers in the Army. The son of a retired brigadier general, he had been handpicked from the Officer Candidate School at Fort Benning by then Major General Matthew B. Ridgway to serve as his aide-de-camp. Faith served with Ridgway throughout Europe and jumped with the 82nd Airborne Division on D-Day. In battle, Faith was intense, fearless, aggressive, and unforgiving of error or caution.

Force Composition

Infantry Units

  • 3rd Battalion, 31st Infantry (3/31) - Companies K, L, and M (This was Dad's battalion)
  • 1st Battalion, 32nd Infantry (1/32) - Companies A, B, and C
  • 2nd Battalion, 31st Infantry (2/31) - Unable to reach the battle in time

Support Units

  • 57th Field Artillery Battalion - Three batteries with 105mm howitzers
  • 31st Tank Company - 22 Sherman tanks (never able to join the main battle due to Chinese roadblock at Hill 1221)
  • D Battery, 15th Antiaircraft Artillery - M19 and M16 vehicles with 40mm guns and .50-cal machine guns

Organizational Challenges

During the Chosin Reservoir campaign, RCT-31's organizational structure reflected the complex, ad hoc nature of military operations in the rapidly evolving Korean War environment. A late-stage order from Major General David G. Barr transferred the 1st Battalion, 31st Infantry from RCT-31 to direct X Corps control, tasking it with guarding the division's flanks rather than supporting the main regimental effort.

This redeployment created substantial logistical and tactical difficulties. The loss of this battalion eliminated crucial rear guard support and disrupted the essential triangular support structure required for effective divisional operations. This reorganization left the forward elements dangerously exposed and undermined the tactical coherence that had been central to RCT-31's original design.

Mission and Deployment

As part of the United Nations Command's final offensive, Major General Edward M. Almond ordered the 7th Infantry Division to provide RCT-31 with orders to secure the area and advance north toward the Yalu River, the border with China, while protecting the Marines' exposed eastern flank.

On November 25–26, the lead elements of RCT-31 (Task Force MacLean), including Faith's 1/32 Infantry, began relieving the 5th Marines on Chosin's east side so the Marines could redeploy to the west side of the reservoir. Due to delays, 1/32 Infantry occupied the forwardmost positions alone, without artillery support, for a full day. The rest of Task Force MacLean arrived on November 27, assembling in three positions along a 10-mile stretch on the reservoir's east side.

Deployment of RCT-31's Units on November 27, 1950

MacLean deployed his RCT-31 forces from north to south in the following order: 1/32 Infantry Battalion, MacLean's forward command post, the 31st Heavy Mortar Company, 3/31 Infantry Battalion, the 57th Field Artillery Battalion (FAB), and the 31st Infantry headquarters with the 31st Tank Company at Hudong-ni. However, the 2/31 Infantry Battalion remained delayed at Pungsan and had not yet departed. This battalion would never link up with the other two units—a failure that would prove significant in the battle ahead.

The map below illustrates the deployment of RCT 31's forces. On the reservoir's eastern flank, the regiment's two infantry battalions held strategic positions. Colonel Faith's 1/32nd Battalion secured the northern sector above an inlet that feeds into the reservoir. This battalion relieved Marine units on November 25, allowing those Marines to relocate to the reservoir's western side. Within the 1/32nd, Companies A, B, and C were entrenched in foxholes arranged in an inverted "U" shape, with a command post situated at the formation's center. When MacLean arrived on November 27 with the remainder of RCT 31's elements, he joined Colonel Faith at the 1/32nd's command post, consolidating the unit's leadership and control of the area.

Arriving on November 27, the 3/31st Battalion—comprised of Companies K, L, and M—set up a circular defensive perimeter on the eastern side of the inlet. Dad, serving with Company K, was assigned to the sector that overlooked the bridge spanning the creek at the inlet's tip. At that point a gravel road from the 1/32nd position intersected south-bound railroad tracks, which ran in front of the bridge and then followed the reservoir's shoreline all the way to Hill 1221 farther to the south. While the rifle companies formed the outer ring of the perimeter, the battalion's support elements—the 57th Field Artillery Battery (FAB), D Company's mortar section, and medical support units—were stationed in the protected interior alongside the battalion's command post.

Further south, just beyond Hill 1221, lay the third position in the modest town of Hudong-ni. Here the RCT-31 headquarters and the 31st Tank Company were sited. The gravel road that had run from the northernmost position (the 1/32nd Battalion) along the shoreline past the 3/31st perimeter turned away from the parallel railroad at Hill 1221, looped around the opposite side of the hill, and entered Hudong-ni. This road served as the sole ingress and egress for the entire area. From Hudong-ni it continued another five miles south to Hagaru-ri, where X Corps headquarters was established beside a newly constructed airstrip. That strip accommodated Marine aircraft that provided close-air support to the Marine units on the reservoir's western shore and to MacLean's RCT-31 on the eastern side.

Chosin Reservoir Area showing positions of Task Force MacLean

Basic defensive positions were established, but because the Americans did not anticipate immediate enemy action, they failed to create a tight perimeter, leaving sizable gaps between the three deployments. The situation deteriorated when Chinese Communist Forces (CCF) erected a roadblock that isolated the 31st Tank Company from the two main infantry elements. Hill 1221, now occupied by the CCF's 80th Division, severed RCT-31's headquarters and the tank company's armor from both the 1/32nd and 3/31st Battalions—a development that proved decisive in the ensuing battle.

Over the next few days the tank company made two attempts to re-establish contact with the northern units; each effort was repulsed, and the resulting wreckage left charred, burned-out tanks strewn across the road around Hill 1221. Eventually, both the 31st Tank Company and RCT-31 headquarters were ordered to withdraw to X Corps headquarters at Hagaru-ri. Lieutenant Colonel Faith—unaware of the recall—had no choice but to order RCT-31 to break out of the Chinese encirclement in an effort to reach Hudong-ni.

"Task Force MacLean faced significant disadvantages compared to the Marines. Many Army soldiers had little combat training, having come from occupation duty in Japan. They were ill-equipped for the extreme cold, lacking the insulated winter gear issued to the Marines. Communications were poor; there was no time to lay landlines, and radio contact was unreliable."

Timeline of the Five-Day Battle: November 27 – December 1, 1950

The following timeline presents a chronological account of the key events during Task Force MacLean/Faith's five-day stand at the Chosin Reservoir. It traces the mounting pressure exerted by overwhelming Chinese forces, the steady erosion of combat power under relentless attacks and extreme cold, and the cascade of command decisions made under fire. Together, these events culminated in the December 1 breakout attempt and the near-total annihilation of Regimental Combat Team 31.

November 27, 2200 Hours

The first night's Chinese assault began with a ferocious attack under cover of darkness. Bugles sounded and troops shouted as the CCF 80th and 81st Divisions moved to encircle the three deployment positions of Task Force MacLean. The initial blows fell on Faith's 1/32 Infantry Battalion along the northern perimeter, where successive waves pressed steadily closer. By 0030 hours, a coordinated assault broke through Company B's outposts. Company A on the left flank was completely overrun before being retaken in a counterattack.

November 28, 0130 Hours

During the same night, the 3/31st Battalion came under heavy attack, with Chinese forces breaching the perimeters of Companies K and L. Company K—Dad's unit—held three platoons along the road directly in front of the bridge at the inlet's mouth. The first wave overwhelmed the company, forcing it to fall back into the central sector defended by Battery A, 57th Field Artillery Battalion. The sudden collapse created chaos; at one point, the 57th's 40 mm guns inadvertently fired into retreating American positions, compounding the confusion.

Dawn, November 28

At first light, Chinese forces withdrew into the surrounding hills to avoid Marine air attacks. Aside from occasional sniper fire, the lull allowed riflemen to reoccupy their foxholes and reestablish a defensive perimeter around the bridge.

The 1/32 Battalion suffered catastrophic losses—more than 100 killed and another 100 wounded. The 3/31 Battalion and the 57th Field Artillery Battalion also sustained heavy casualties, and the 31st Medical Company was virtually destroyed. The ground was littered with American and Chinese dead.

Although the battalion command post came under fire, it remained operational. One platoon from Company K on a hill and a platoon from Company I on an adjacent rise had been overrun. Colonel Riley, the senior officer of the 3/31st, was wounded, along with many others. Orders were issued to tighten the perimeter and consolidate remaining forces.

November 28, 1800 Hours

The second night of CCF assaults began early. This time, the 3/31st Battalion was better prepared, supported by more accurate and sustained 40 mm fire from the 57th Field Artillery. Most perimeter breaches were sealed, though Chinese pressure continued throughout the night. The 1/32 Battalion, still without artillery support and relying solely on rifle companies, suffered heavy casualties under continued enemy attacks.

November 29, 0200 Hours

Recognizing the exposed position of the 1/32 Battalion, Colonel MacLean ordered a withdrawal south to the 3/31 perimeter, directing that all weapons and wounded be brought along. Vehicles were disabled, wounded were loaded into trucks, and the movement began at 0500 hours.

November 29, Dawn

Under intense enemy fire, the 1/32 Battalion fought its way into the 3/31 perimeter. Amid the confusion, Colonel MacLean mistook a group of Chinese troops for the delayed 2/31 Battalion. Believing they were being fired upon by the 3/31st, he ran toward them shouting, "Those are my boys!" He was shot multiple times, captured, and later died in Chinese captivity.

With MacLean incapacitated, Lieutenant Colonel Don Faith, commander of the 1/32 Battalion, assumed overall command. The combined force thereafter became known as Task Force Faith.

November 29, Daytime

Faith consolidated the remaining units into a single defensive position. Marine aircraft provided close air support and conducted supply drops. Search parties attempted to locate Colonel MacLean but failed. Weather conditions worsened, with temperatures falling to −30°F amid heavy snowfall.

November 29, Nighttime

After two nights of intense assaults failed to break Task Force Faith, the third night was comparatively quiet. Only sporadic small-arms fire and occasional mortar rounds fell as Chinese forces paused to reorganize and bring forward additional mortar teams. Resupply efforts continued by air, but many bundles landed outside the perimeter and fell into enemy hands, including critically needed 40 mm ammunition.

November 30, Daytime

Major General David Barr arrived by helicopter with orders for all X Corps elements to withdraw to Hagaru-ri. Task Force Faith, now under Marine operational control, would receive air support but would have to fight its way out overland while carrying more than 600 wounded.

November 30, 2000 Hours

On the fourth night, Chinese forces launched their heaviest attack yet, reinforced with heavy mortars and light artillery. Continuous bombardment battered the perimeter until dawn. Mortars of all calibers—up to 120 mm—saturated the position. The task force's artillery battery was nearly out of ammunition.

Although severe losses were inflicted on the enemy, Task Force Faith suffered more than 100 additional casualties. Commanders concluded that another major assault could not be sustained. Unlike previous nights, Chinese forces did not withdraw at daylight, instead remaining in close proximity and continuing their bombardment. Aid stations were overwhelmed by the volume of wounded.

December 1, 1100 Hours

The 1st Marine Division, now exercising overall control of X Corps from Hagaru-ri, ordered Task Force Faith to break out. Lieutenant Colonel Faith scheduled the departure for 1300 hours. All nonessential equipment—including tanks, trucks, radios, and the howitzers of the 57th Field Artillery—was destroyed after firing final rounds to prevent capture.

Faith planned to move south along the snow-covered road skirting Hill 1221 toward RCT-31 headquarters at Hudong-ni, where he expected support from the 31st Tank Company. Unknown to him—and not disclosed by General Barr—the tank company and headquarters elements had already withdrawn to Hagaru-ri. Faith was effectively on his own, tasked with fighting ten miles south along the reservoir.

December 1, 1300 Hours

The breakout column departed with 22 vehicles carrying approximately 600 wounded. The 1/32 Battalion led, followed by the 57th Field Artillery, the Heavy Mortar Company, and the 3/31 Battalion as rear guard. Almost immediately, the column came under heavy fire from concealed Chinese positions.

December 1, Afternoon

A tragic friendly-fire incident occurred when four Marine aircraft mistakenly struck the lead elements with napalm. The attack caused immediate casualties and threw the forward units into disarray. Despite this, the column continued south until reaching a destroyed bridge two miles ahead, where it encountered a Chinese roadblock at the base of Hill 1221. As the road curved southeast along the hill, the convoy came under fire from both Hill 1221 and higher ground on the opposite side.

December 1, Late Afternoon

Concluding that the situation was untenable, Lieutenant Colonel Faith led a desperate assault up Hill 1221 with several hundred men, including wounded volunteers. Despite heavy losses, most of the hill was seized. Many survivors, however, crossed the frozen reservoir independently rather than rejoining the column. During close-quarters fighting, Faith was severely wounded by grenade fragments.

December 1, 2100 Hours

The next senior officer assumed command and pushed the convoy into Hudong-ni, unaware that RCT-31 elements had already withdrawn. Chinese forces now occupied the town. From concealed positions, they shot the drivers of the first three trucks and then overran the remaining vehicles. The task force collapsed. Survivors scattered onto the frozen reservoir. Lieutenant Colonel Don Faith died of his wounds in the cab of a truck. By midnight, Hudong-ni contained only the dead and the severely wounded.

This timeline captures the principal events of the five-day battle. The sections that follow expand on these moments, drawing on after-action reports, survivor accounts, and historical records to convey the full scale of the fighting and the conditions endured by Task Force MacLean/Faith.

Initial Chinese Attacks: Night of November 27-28

The red arrows on the map below represent CCP attack routes and highlight the precarious position of the American forces. By November 27, Chinese Communist Forces (CCF) had completely surrounded the troops on every side of the Chosin Reservoir, seizing the dominant high ground and doing so largely unnoticed. Their operational plan called for coordinated, full-force assaults aimed at annihilating the entire X Corps: the Marines entrenched on the reservoir's western shore and the Army units positioned on the eastern side.

The Chinese strategy hinged on keeping the Americans isolated through relentless night-time attacks, pinning them in place and preventing any breakout from the encirclement. Even if a retreat were attempted, the CCF intended to block the only viable escape corridor—the road leading south to Hagaru-ri, where a modest airstrip and X Corps headquarters offered the sole evacuation point. To ensure total containment, they also planned a thrust south of Hagaru-ri to sever that corridor entirely, cutting off the only route to the sea in the event of a mass withdrawal.

CCP Encirclement of X Corps and Attack Routes

At 2200 hours on November 27, Chinese divisions launched their ferocious attack under cover of darkness. Soldiers blowing bugles and screaming wildly sowed confusion and fear as the CCF 80th and 81st Divisions encircled the unsuspecting units of Task Force MacLean.

Almond's Visit and Misjudgment: November 28

In a surreal moment amid the chaos, Major General Almond arrived at the 1/32's command post that morning, accompanied by his aide, First Lieutenant Alexander Haig, to assess the dire situation. Despite overwhelming evidence of massive Chinese intervention, Almond exhorted the soldiers to stick with the original order begin the northern offensive.

"The enemy who is delaying you for the moment is nothing more than remnants of Chinese divisions fleeing north. We're still attacking and we're going all the way to the Yalu. Don't let a bunch of Chinese laundrymen stop you."

Before departing, Almond awarded Lieutenant Colonel Faith the Silver Star for gallantry. Yet, the gesture rang hollow, as he offered no relief from the desperate circumstances and continued to insist on an attack. Disillusioned and frustrated by his commander's lack of understanding, Faith tossed the Silver Star into the snow after Almond's departure.

Almond could not have been more mistaken. The so-called "remnants" were, in reality, tens of thousands of fresh, well-coordinated Chinese troops who had already encircled much of the task force. Almond's dismissive attitude and failure to grasp the scale of the Chinese intervention left Task Force MacLean/Faith dangerously exposed and woefully unprepared for the onslaught that had already begun.

Second Night Attack: November 28-29

At around midnight on 29 November, the CCF 80th Division attacked Task Force MacLean once again. The fighting was savage, often hand to hand. Chinese prisoners indicated involvement of the 80th Division and elements of the 81st Division. The Chinese attacked with North Korean tanks and self-propelled guns, which were destroyed by air strikes and infantry weapons.

For twelve hours beginning around 1800 on the 28th, Chinese forces probed the lower perimeter but made no lasting penetrations. High casualties in Companies K and L, however, forced the two units to combine. This night reminds me of what Dad told me about the continuous waves of Chinese attackers. Subsequent waves would pick up the guns of the fallen and encroach closer and closer to the front line. By 0600 on November 29, the enemy had withdrawn, but ammunition and supplies had reached critically low levels.

Weather conditions deteriorated rapidly with temperatures plunging to -30 degrees F (-34 C) and heavy snow falling, impeding mobility and causing several men to freeze to death in their foxholes. The defenders found themselves completely cut off, with no possibility of resupply except through dangerous airdrops, many of which fell in enemy territory.

Death of Colonel MacLean: November 29

At around 0200, MacLean, still in the 1/32 perimeter, ordered the battalion to withdraw south in the darkness to the 3/31's perimeter, taking all weapons and wounded with them. After disabling and abandoning several vehicles and loading the wounded into trucks, MacLean, Faith, and the 1/32 began moving south at 0500.

By dawn, the battalion reached the 3/31 perimeter, only to find it under heavy enemy attack. Lieutenant Colonel Faith led a party of men that successfully drove the CCF off the bridge and cleared the block in front of Dad's Company K position. MacLean then came forward in his jeep. He spotted a column of troops whom he believed was his overdue 2/31 battalion.

"MacLean, believing they were American, ran towards them, shouting, 'Those are my boys.' He dashed out onto the frozen reservoir towards the perimeter, attempting to stop what he believed was friendly fire."

The troops who opened fire were indeed Chinese Communist Forces. As the CCF soldiers concealed themselves near the bridge, they suddenly unleashed a burst of gunfire on Colonel MacLean, striking him multiple times. His men could only watch in horror as an enemy combatant seized the wounded officer and dragged him into the surrounding brush. MacLean succumbed to his injuries four days later while in Chinese captivity, becoming the second—and ultimately the last—American regimental commander killed during the Korean War. Given that Company K, Dad's unit, held the sector directly opposite the bridge, it is plausible that he witnessed—or at least heard—the chaotic scene in which the colonel was captured.

At that moment, with the capture and subsequent death of Colonel MacLean, RCT-31 known as Task Force MacLean became Task Force Faith.

Faith Takes Command and Consolidation

Once inside the perimeter, Lieutenant Colonel Faith surveyed the carnage. Hundreds of American and CCF dead littered the ground. The 3/31 had suffered over 300 casualties and its L Company had ceased to exist. With MacLean gone, Lieutenant Colonel Faith now took command and did his best to strengthen the perimeter, consolidating the task force into one defensive position as the Chinese intensified their attacks.

Marine air controller Captain Stamford, who was attached to RCT-31, radioed for close-air support from the Marines and requested an airdrop of urgently needed supplies. In response, Colonel Faith dispatched search parties to locate the missing Colonel MacLean, but none of the teams were able to find him.

Air Support Operations

The withdrawal of 1/32 was carried out under the protective cover of Marine and Navy aircraft. Captain Stamford expertly coordinated both supply drops from cargo planes and close air support missions, filling the sky with transport aircraft, parachutes, and F4U Corsairs. However, these aircraft could only operate during daylight hours, which partly explains why Chinese forces often seized territory at night but then withdrew to previous positions during the day to avoid devastating air attacks.

Each night the fighting wore down RCT-31 even further, draining ammunition, manpower and morale. Exhausted soldiers huddled against the bitter cold while wave after wave of enemy attacks surged through the darkness, leaving little time for rest and ever-diminishing supplies. The relentless threat of being overrun, the piercing frost, and mounting casualties forged an atmosphere of unrelenting fear and desperation. I still remember asking Dad about his wartime experience; a chill ran up my arm as he described the endless streams of attackers that seemed to never cease. It was one of the few moments he was willing to share about that harrowing battle.

Orders to Withdraw: November 30

At roughly 20:00 hours, the Chinese Communist Forces launched their fourth successive night assault. As darkness deepened, the attack grew more ferocious, now bolstered by heavy mortars and light artillery. Wave after wave of enemy troops hammered the perimeter throughout the long, bitter night; only a handful managed to breach the defenses, and each was promptly cut down. Although Task Force Faith inflicted severe losses on the Chinese, the engagement cost the unit an additional 100 casualties, leading its commanders to conclude that they could not withstand another major offensive.

On November 30, Major General Dave Barr, commander of the 7th Infantry Division, arrived by helicopter bearing grim orders for Colonel Faith. Every X Corps element—including Task Force Faith, now operating under Marine command—was instructed to withdraw to X Corps headquarters at Hagaru-ri. While the Marines would continue to furnish air support, the withdrawing troops would have to fend for themselves on the ground, a daunting prospect compounded by the growing number of wounded they were forced to carry.

The Breakout Attempt: December 1, 1950

By December 1 1950 the American forces were on the brink of collapse. Ammunition stocks were critically depleted, and more than half of the troops were either killed or wounded—among them a disproportionate number of senior leaders. Confronted with encirclement and overwhelming odds, Lieutenant Colonel Don Faith had no viable option but to launch a desperate breakout southward toward the Marine lines at Hagaru-ri.

At 1100 hours, the commander of the 1st Marine Division issued the fateful order for a southward breakout. Lieutenant Colonel Don Faith gathered his remaining officers and set a departure time of 1300 hours. The situation was so dire that only the bare essentials were kept: enough vehicles to evacuate roughly 600 wounded soldiers and minimal personal gear. All other materiel was deliberately destroyed on site—tanks, trucks, radios, and even the artillery's howitzers were blown up after firing their final rounds—to prevent anything useful from falling into enemy hands.

The Column Formation

The breakout column was led by the 1/32 Battalion, now commanded by Major Crosby P. Miller. Behind it followed the 57th Field Artillery Battalion, the Heavy Mortar Company, and the 3/31 Battalion, which included Dad's Company K. At 1300 hours on December 1 the column departed, spearheaded by a twin-mounted 40 mm gun vehicle, with the remnants of the battalion bringing up the rear.

The convoy—carrying roughly 600 wounded soldiers crammed into trucks—trudged south along the snow-slick gravel road that clung to the reservoir's edge. Enemy fire swept the surrounding hills, forcing the men to scramble for any bit of cover they could find. In his after-action report, Captain Kitz, commander of Company K, noted that maintaining any coherent formation was virtually impossible; the troops were compelled to huddle behind the trucks for protection and, whenever the fire intensified, to dash toward the reservoir's shoreline for additional shelter. Because Captain Kitz was dad's commander, it is highly probable that Dad experienced what Kitz describes in this account. (To read Kitz's full seven-page personal statement, select the "Company K Story" link in the menu above.)

Faith kept the breakout column tightly packed, limiting the convoy to just twenty-two vehicles to transport the wounded. Marine F4U Corsairs and Navy F7F twin-engine fighters delivered vital close-air support, strafing and bombing Chinese positions as the heavily burdened American column inched southward along the gravel road on the eastern shore of the Chosin Reservoir.

Friendly Fire Incident

Almost immediately after moving out, the column was hit by withering small-arms and automatic-weapon fire from concealed Chinese positions. The crisis escalated when four Marine pilots miscalculated their napalm runs and inadvertently struck the lead American troops instead of the enemy. The resulting fire engulfed several soldiers, killing them instantly, and threw the forward companies into chaos as they scattered to escape the very air support meant to protect them.

The accidental napalm strike dealt a crushing blow to morale across the entire task force. After briefly rallying his men, Colonel Faith pressed the column forward, pushing past sporadic Chinese fire teams. By mid-afternoon the convoy reached a ruined bridge roughly two miles south of its point of departure, forcing the exhausted troops to confront yet another obstacle on their desperate breakout.

Hill 1221: The Final Stand

Below the bridge, the road climbed sharply onto the lower slopes of Hill 1221 before looping into a tight hairpin turn that sent it winding southwest around the hill's far side. As the convoy squeezed through this exposed corridor, Chinese small-arms and machine-gun fire poured in from the front and the flanks, tearing apart trucks and forcing the entire column to grind to a halt. The terrain left the convoy utterly vulnerable, making it an easy target for the enemy's fire.

Faith recognized that Hill 1221 commanded the surrounding terrain and that the Chinese 242nd Regiment had entrenched a strong defensive line there. Seizing the hill was the only realistic way to break the encirclement. He rallied the remaining troops—wounded men who could still shoulder rifles—and prepared to assault the position. When two Republic of Korea soldiers refused to join the attack, Faith used his sidearm to enforce discipline, ending their resistance. The episode underscored the desperate and brutal conditions the unit faced as it struggled to push forward.

"In a desperate charge, several hundred men—including many of the wounded, who preferred to die attacking rather than waiting helplessly in trucks, charged up Hill 1221."

Despite the heavy casualties, Faith's men managed to wrest most of Hill 1221 from the Chinese 242nd Regiment. Yet, after cresting the summit, many of the surviving attackers did not regroup with the main column. Instead, they slipped down the far side of the hill, crossed the frozen surface of the reservoir, and began moving toward Hagaru-ri on their own.

Faith personally led subsequent assaults on the remaining roadblocks but was severely wounded by enemy grenade fragments during the attack. Major Robert E. Jones, the intelligence officer of the 1/32 Battalion, took charge of what remained of the column.

The Breakout Route and Hill 1221, December 1, 1950

Task Force Disintegration

As night fell and the protective Marine air cover withdrew, Chinese infantry attacks grew increasingly bold. The fragmentation of the force during the Hill 1221 assault—combined with mounting casualties that swept through every level of command, from platoon leaders up to task-force headquarters—brought the unit to the brink of collapse. Apart from the severely wounded, Jones was left with fewer than 200 combat-effective soldiers.

Almost every truck in the column was immobilized—flat tires riddled the fleet, and several vehicles were beyond repair. Jones was only able to get about fifteen trucks back in operation, far fewer than needed to evacuate all the wounded. Faced with this stark shortage, he made the painful decision to leave a small guard detail with the wounded who now had no transport, then press onward southward, hoping that Marine forces at Hagaru-ri might later rescue the abandoned men.

Beyond the hairpin turn, two charred tanks from the earlier 31st Tank Company action lay half-buried in the road, narrowing the passage. The convoy moved without further incident until about 2100 hours, when it reached Hudong-ni—roughly midway between Hill 1221 and Hagaru-ri. From within the village, Chinese fire struck the column hard: the drivers of the first three trucks were killed, and the remaining vehicles and troops were swept by a hail of small-arms fire, delivering the final, decisive blow to the already exhausted withdrawing column. Everyone who could move scattered, most heading for the frozen reservoir.

Captain Kitz's after-action report paints a stark, survival-driven dilemma for the men still able to move: remain with the wounded and almost certainly die, or abandon them and attempt a perilous crossing of the ice. With ammunition exhausted, Kitz ordered the remaining soldiers in his company—most likely including Dad—to leave the road and slip onto the frozen surface of the reservoir, hoping to reach the Marine lines a few miles to the south.

By midnight, the village of Hudong-ni was left with only the dead and the seriously wounded. Among those stranded was Lieutenant Colonel Don Faith, who succumbed to his injuries in the cab of a two-ton truck. He was later posthumously awarded the Medal of Honor for his extraordinary leadership during the desperate breakout.

Aftermath, Survivors and Impact

The Rescue Operations

During the night of December 1-2, 1950, survivors of RCT-31 began reaching Marine lines at Hagaru-ri. Most straggled in over the next three days, nearly all crossing the frozen reservoir on foot. The Chinese appeared to consider those who reached the ice as effectively out of the fight and largely left them unmolested. At Hudong-ni, Chinese forces even administered aid to some wounded Americans before releasing them.

Lieutenant Colonel Olin L. Beall, commanding the 1st Marine Division's Motor Transport Battalion, led a daring rescue mission across the frozen reservoir by jeep, recovering over 300 soldiers—many suffering from wounds, frostbite, and shock. Additional Marine patrols rescued several hundred more the following day.

Casualty Assessment

The human cost of Task Force Faith's destruction was staggering. Of approximately 2,500 U.S. troops originally encircled north of the Chinese roadblock, roughly 1,500 ultimately reached friendly lines. However, only about 1,050 made it to Hagaru-ri, and of those, just 385 were classified as fit for continued combat duty. The remainder were wounded, frostbitten, or in a state of physical and psychological collapse.

Over 1,000 soldiers of Task Force Faith were killed in action or died in captivity, representing nearly 40 percent of the task force's original strength. This catastrophic loss made RCT-31 one of the most severely mauled units in U.S. military history.

Chinese losses were equally devastating. Though precise figures remain unknown, both the 80th and 81st Divisions of the People's Volunteer Army sustained such heavy casualties that neither appeared on the battlefield again until April 1951. Chinese sources claim 4,300 killed or seriously wounded, not counting those who froze to death due to inadequate winter clothing.

Reorganization and Continued Service

Following the destruction of Task Force Faith, the 385 able-bodied survivors, which included Dad, were rapidly reorganized with other 7th Infantry Division elements to form Provisional Battalion 31/7, which was then attached to the 7th Marines. The provisional battalion was organized into two composite battalions, each containing three rifle companies.

During the 1st Marine Division's withdrawal to Hungnam, the 31/7 fought alongside the Marines, helping to secure the route and protect the column as it moved south. Despite facing continued enemy attacks and severe winter conditions, the battalion demonstrated remarkable resilience and combat effectiveness throughout the withdrawal.

Upon reaching the port of Hungnam, the survivors were evacuated by sea along with the rest of the U.N. forces during the final phase of the Hungnam Evacuation in December 1950. The remnants of the 7th Infantry Division were subsequently reconstituted and returned to combat operations in Korea in early 1951.

Legacy and Historical Truth

Historical Misrepresentation and Vindication

For fifty years following Chosin, RCT-31's performance was consistently mischaracterized by military leaders and historians. High-ranking officers, including 1st Marine Division commander General O.P. Smith, interpreted the arrival of weaponless survivors as evidence of cowardice and unit collapse. General Smith's dismissive reference to them as "Army jokers" exemplified this unfair assessment.

This harsh judgment ignored the brutal reality of RCT-31's situation. Soldiers who survived the destruction of their positions were forced to escape on foot—more than half carrying wounds—after exhausting their ammunition in fierce fighting. The weaponless condition of RCT-31 survivors became a source of undeserved criticism, failing to recognize that retaining empty weapons during a desperate escape across frozen terrain served no tactical purpose.

Official Secrecy and Suppression

Following the battle, all records and personal statements were classified as secret in an effort to conceal the full truth of the Chosin disaster. Key Army documents, especially those detailing the rapid and fatal redeployment of RCT-31, were classified as "Secret" and withheld from public scrutiny for decades.

The silence was both bureaucratic and personal. Survivors returned home to a society eager to move past war, often finding little understanding or acknowledgment of their ordeal. Many veterans bore physical and emotional scars in isolation, their stories largely unspoken for years. The Korean War became known as "the forgotten war," and the heroism and suffering at Chosin remained largely unrecognized outside military circles.

The Truth Emerges

Eventually, the release of Chinese military documents and scholarly research—including work by Marine Corps Major Patrick Parrott and historian Roy Appleman—reshaped the narrative. Chinese records confirmed that RCT-31 was vastly outnumbered and under-resourced, and that its actions significantly diverted pressure from the Marines, enabling an orderly withdrawal.

It took decades for many records to be declassified and for the full Chosin story to emerge. Only after the release of thousands of pages of Marine Corps records and personal testimonies did the public begin to grasp the scale of the ordeal and the extraordinary resilience shown by the "Chosin Few."

"Today, the battle is recognized as one of the most harrowing episodes in Korean War history. Their sacrifice stands as a monument to individual courage and unit cohesion in the face of systemic challenges often beyond their control, embodying the finest traditions of American military service under the most adverse conditions imaginable."

Mission Accomplishment and Strategic Value

For decades, RCT-31's story remained largely overlooked, with many perceiving the task force's collapse as shameful for the U.S. Army. However, deeper examination reveals that RCT-31 accomplished key aspects of its mission. For five days, the task force successfully guarded the right flank of the 1st Marine Division, protecting it from Chinese attacks.

Historians now largely agree that this effort prevented the Chinese 80th and 81st Divisions from seizing Hagaru-ri—a critical Marine base and airstrip—before Marine units could concentrate sufficient defensive forces. RCT-31's presence may well have prevented the encirclement and annihilation of the Marines, while the task force's actions resulted in the effective destruction of the Chinese 80th Division.

Closing Thoughts About Dad's Korean War Experience

War really is hell, and what Dad went through was horrific and terrifying beyond imagination. The Korean War, often called the "Forgotten War," was anything but forgettable for the men who fought in its brutal conditions. Dad's experience at the Frozen Chosin represents one of the most harrowing chapters in American military history, where temperatures plummeted to minus 35 degrees Fahrenheit and Chinese forces surrounded and attacked our troops in overwhelming numbers.

As one of only 385 able-bodied survivors from what became known as the "Frozen Chosin" battle, Dad witnessed firsthand the devastating toll of modern warfare. The psychological weight of survival, knowing that so many others didn't make it home, must have been enormous. Survivor's guilt—a common and deeply painful experience among combat veterans—likely haunted him long after the guns fell silent.

While the term PTSD wasn't known or widely recognized at the time, there is little doubt that Dad, like so many others who endured the crucible of Chosin, was deeply traumatized by the ordeal. The invisible wounds of war—anxiety, nightmares, and the relentless replaying of traumatic memories—would have accompanied him long after he returned home. His survival was an extraordinary feat, but it came at a profound emotional cost that shaped the rest of his life.

For decades after the war, the Army units that fought at Frozen Chosin faced an additional burden beyond their physical and psychological wounds: they were unjustly branded as cowards who had fled in the face of the enemy. This cruel mischaracterization added insult to the grievous injuries these brave men had already endured. The truth about their heroic stand against overwhelming odds, their strategic fighting withdrawal that saved countless lives, and their crucial role in allowing the Marine evacuation at Hungnam Harbor was buried beneath layers of military politics and incomplete historical accounts.

Only in recent years has the full truth about the Army's role at Frozen Chosin begun to emerge. Modern historians and military analysts have finally recognized that these soldiers didn't retreat in cowardice—they conducted one of the most remarkable fighting withdrawals in military history, holding off vastly superior Chinese forces while protecting the eastern flank of the UN withdrawal.

The fact that Dad survived the Frozen Chosin and continued to serve with distinction until his discharge speaks to the extraordinary character of his generation. These men faced unimaginable hardships with courage and determination, often at great personal cost. They bore not only the physical and psychological wounds of battle, but also the unjust shame of being misunderstood and maligned by their own country.

In reflecting on Dad's experience, we must remember that behind every military statistic and historical account stands a human being who faced fear, pain, and loss with remarkable bravery. His story is not just one of survival, but of service, sacrifice, and the enduring strength of the human spirit in the face of unthinkable adversity.

The Korean War may be called "forgotten," but Dad's service and the service of his fellow veterans should never be forgotten. Their legacy lives on in the freedom we enjoy today and in the notable example they set for future generations about duty, honor, and the true cost of liberty.

I have written this story not just to honor Dad's memory, but to ensure that my children and their children's children will always know the extraordinary man whose courage and sacrifice helped shape the world they inherited. In a time when heroes are often fictional, they should know that a real hero in their family heritage walked among us—one who faced the unimaginable with quiet dignity and never spoke of his own bravery.

This is their grandfather's story, their great-grandfather's legacy.
