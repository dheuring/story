Five Days of Hell

The timeline above provides the chronological framework. What follows is a more detailed account of the five-day battle, focusing particularly on the experiences of Company K—my father's unit—and the broader tactical picture necessary to understand the full scope of the disaster.

Initial Chinese Assault

The red arrows on the map below represent Chinese Communist Forces (CCF) attack routes and highlight the precarious position of the American forces. By November 27, the CCF had completely surrounded the troops on every side of the Chosin Reservoir, seizing the dominant high ground and doing so largely unnoticed. Their operational plan called for coordinated, full-force assaults aimed at annihilating the entire X Corps: the 1st Marine Division on the western shore and RCT-31 (the Army's 31st Regimental Combat Team) on the eastern side. RCT-31 included American infantry, artillery, and support units, along with integrated Republic of Korea (ROK) soldiers who served in various roles throughout the task force.

The Chosin Reservoir, roughly 8 miles long, separated the Marine and Army positions—Marines to the west, RCT-31 to the east, with only a single road connecting each side to Hagaru-ri to the south. The Chinese strategy hinged on keeping the Americans isolated through relentless nighttime attacks, pinning them in place and preventing any breakout from the encirclement. Even if a retreat were attempted, the CCF intended to block the only viable escape corridor—the road leading south to Hagaru-ri, where the 1st Marine Division headquarters, the modest airstrip, and a supply base offered the sole evacuation point. To ensure total containment, they also planned a thrust south of Hagaru-ri to cut off the road at Koto-ri and beyond, severing the route to the port at Hungnam in the event of a mass withdrawal.

Company K: The Battle Begins

The following account narrows the focus from the broader strategic picture to the specific experiences of Company K—my father's unit—during the first night's assault. Drawing from Captain Richard J. Kitz's after-action report, this narrative provides insight into the chaos, confusion, and terror that defined the initial Chinese attack on the 3/31st Battalion's positions.

Company K had arrived at the Chosin Reservoir at approximately 1600 hours on November 27, immediately establishing defensive positions on the eastern side of the reservoir's northern inlet overlooking the bridge. Captain Kitz deployed his three rifle platoons across a broad defensive area:

  • Two squads of the 1st Platoon, commanded by Lieutenant McFarland, were positioned on the trail east of the bridge.
  • The 3rd Platoon held positions on a ridge line.
  • The 2nd Platoon extended further down the ridge, connecting with the 3rd Platoon on one side and Company I's positions on the other.
  • A weapons squad with one machine gun was stationed at the base of the ridge.
  • A machine gun section from Company I covered the draw between positions.

At approximately 1900 hours, Captain Kitz reported to the 3/31 Battalion command post, where Colonel Riley issued orders for a northward attack the following morning. These orders reflected Operations Order O-25, issued by Colonel MacLean that morning, which directed an aggressive attack north toward the Yalu River. The operational plan called for the 3/31 Battalion to seize Objective A and protect the regiment's eastern flank before continuing the northward advance. Company K was to advance along the ridge line while Company L moved north along the road, with Company I held in reserve. It was a routine briefing for what appeared to be a routine operation. None of them knew that within hours, the Chinese would launch a devastating assault that would shatter their plans and nearly destroy the battalion.

First Night: November 27-28

At 2200 hours, the carefully laid plans for the next morning's attack became irrelevant. Chinese divisions launched their ferocious assault under cover of darkness. The sound of bugles and shouting voices echoed across the frozen landscape as the CCF 80th and 81st Divisions moved to encircle Task Force MacLean's three deployment positions. On the eastern side of the inlet, Company K—my father's unit—faced the full force of the initial onslaught.

Around 0100 hours, Captain Kitz heard intermittent gunfire and immediately called Lieutenant McFarland to determine its source. Between 0130 and 0200 hours, the sporadic firing suddenly erupted into intense combat near the machine gun section's position. As Kitz hurriedly put on his boots to investigate, the situation exploded into chaos—heavy gunfire erupted at close range. The Chinese assault had reached the company command post, and the CP itself was under fire.

The sudden violence threw the rear area into complete disorder. Cooks emerged from their tents in confusion, and several ROK soldiers working in the kitchen area began to flee in panic. Captain Kitz ran approximately 50 yards to stop the retreating soldiers and attempted to rally them. As he worked to restore order, more men came streaming over a nearby rise—a mixture of ROK soldiers and American troops, all falling back under intense Chinese pressure.

Kitz attempted to organize a defensive line and get the men to return fire, but the situation had deteriorated beyond control. In the darkness, with muzzle flashes lighting the night and soldiers running in all directions, it became impossible to distinguish between retreating ROK troops and attacking Chinese forces. Friend and foe were intermixed in the chaos, making organized resistance nearly impossible. Adding to the confusion, the 57th Field Artillery's guns inadvertently fired into retreating American positions during the height of the assault.

At this critical moment, Lieutenant Simpson, the battalion communications officer, arrived and informed Kitz that Colonel Riley had been wounded and that all officers should report to the battalion command post. Fighting their way through Chinese soldiers who had infiltrated the area, Kitz and Simpson made their way down the road toward the CP. The scene they encountered there was equally chaotic—the command post itself was under attack, with Chinese forces having penetrated deep into the American positions.

Catastrophic Losses and Desperate Defense

The first night's fighting devastated Company K's leadership structure. When Captain Kitz reached the battalion command post, he learned the full extent of the disaster. Lieutenant McFarland and virtually his entire 1st Platoon—approximately 50 men—had been overrun and destroyed. The 2nd Platoon commander, Lieutenant Everest, was killed in action. Lieutenant Smith of the 3rd Platoon was seriously wounded. Of Company K's four officers, only Captain Kitz and one other lieutenant remained unwounded and capable of command.

The battalion's situation was equally grim. Colonel Riley, though wounded, maintained command. However, the Chinese assault had achieved devastating effect across all the task force positions. The defensive perimeters that had seemed adequate in daylight proved woefully insufficient against the coordinated night attack by forces that held every advantage of terrain, numbers, and surprise.

Throughout the night, Chinese forces continued their attacks in waves. American defenders fought desperately, but the extreme cold compounded every challenge. Weapons jammed from the freezing temperatures. Wounded men faced a stark choice between remaining in position where they might freeze to death or attempting to reach aid stations through areas crawling with enemy soldiers. Many who were wounded during the night attacks died from exposure before they could receive medical treatment.

Captain Kitz spent the remainder of the night attempting to reorganize Company K's scattered survivors and establish a cohesive defensive position. In the darkness, this proved nearly impossible. Small groups of soldiers fought isolated battles across the ridgelines and draws. Some units maintained their positions; others fell back in disorder. By dawn on November 28, Company K—which had arrived at the reservoir the previous afternoon as a fully functional infantry company—had lost approximately half its strength and existed only as scattered fragments desperately trying to survive until daylight.

The chaos of the first night's fighting is reflected in the 3rd Battalion's operations log. At 0400 hours on November 28, the battalion reported it had lost contact with Company K. Contact was recovered fifteen minutes later at 0415 hours. This brief notation in the official record captures the confusion and fragmentation that characterized the defensive battle—even battalion headquarters temporarily lost track of entire companies in the darkness and disorder of the Chinese assault.

Reference: 31st RCT Journal (S-1 Log), Untaek, Korea, pages 56–59, November–December 1950, declassified under Authority NND 745074, National Archives.

Day Two: November 28, 1950

Dawn on November 28 revealed the full extent of the previous night's disaster. The three RCT-31 perimeters were all under siege, with Chinese forces occupying commanding positions on the surrounding hills. The task force had suffered catastrophic casualties, lost much of its equipment, and seen its command structure severely damaged. The 1/32 Battalion alone had suffered more than 100 killed and another 100 wounded. The 3/31 Battalion and the 57th Field Artillery Battalion also sustained heavy casualties, and the 31st Medical Company was virtually destroyed. Any hope of continuing the northward attack had evaporated. The only question now was survival.

Morning Assessment and Attempts to Reorganize

As daylight broke, Captain Kitz surveyed what remained of Company K. The scene was devastating. Bodies of American and Chinese soldiers lay scattered across the frozen ground. Equipment was strewn everywhere—abandoned weapons, overturned vehicles, destroyed tents. Of the company that had numbered over 150 men the previous day, fewer than half remained combat-effective. Many were wounded. All were exhausted from the night's fighting and the bone-numbing cold.

Kitz established a command post in a partially destroyed building and began the grim task of reorganizing his company. He consolidated the survivors of the three rifle platoons into two composite platoons, redistributed ammunition and weapons, and attempted to establish a coherent defensive perimeter. The men dug in as best they could—the frozen ground made proper entrenchment nearly impossible, so they piled rocks and used frozen corpses to create crude breastworks.

Throughout the morning, sporadic Chinese fire continued from the surrounding hills. Occasionally, a sniper would find a target. More often, mortar rounds fell into the perimeter, causing further casualties and keeping the defenders constantly on edge. American artillery attempted to suppress the Chinese positions, but ammunition was already running low, and resupply seemed unlikely given the Chinese control of the surrounding terrain.

Company K had been decimated in the first night's assault. My father, serving in one of the rifle platoons, survived the chaos—the close fighting, the desperate withdrawal under fire toward the artillery positions, and the long, tense wait for dawn. It was likely during this first night, when his position was overrun, that he experienced one of the most harrowing moments of the battle: playing dead while enemy soldiers prodded him with rifle butts as they moved through the area checking bodies. This was only the beginning of a five-day ordeal that would drive these men to the outer limits of human endurance.

Almond's Visit and Misjudgment: November 28

In a surreal moment amid the chaos, Major General Almond arrived by light aircraft at the 1/32 Battalion's command post that morning, accompanied by his aide, First Lieutenant Alexander Haig. Both Colonel MacLean and Lieutenant Colonel Faith were present. Despite overwhelming evidence of massive Chinese intervention, Almond exhorted the soldiers to stick with the original order to begin the northern offensive.

"The enemy who is delaying you for the moment is nothing more than remnants of Chinese divisions fleeing north. We're still attacking and we're going all the way to the Yalu. Don't let a bunch of Chinese laundrymen stop you."

Before departing, Almond awarded Lieutenant Colonel Faith the Silver Star for gallantry. Yet the gesture rang hollow, as he offered no relief from the desperate circumstances and continued to insist on an attack. Disillusioned and frustrated by his commander's lack of understanding, Faith tossed the Silver Star into the snow after Almond's departure.

Almond could not have been more mistaken. The so-called "remnants" were, in reality, tens of thousands of fresh, well-coordinated Chinese troops who had already encircled much of the task force. Almond's dismissive attitude and failure to grasp the scale of the Chinese intervention left Task Force MacLean/Faith dangerously exposed and woefully unprepared for the onslaught that had already begun.

Second Night: November 28-29

Any belief that the enemy consisted only of scattered remnants proved catastrophically wrong. The second night of fighting began before darkness had fully settled. As Chinese forces pressed in along the perimeter, a machine-gun position held by Company K was overrun, severing communications almost immediately. By that point, Captain Kitz had fewer than sixty men remaining in the company. With radios destroyed and command and control fractured, Company K fought in near isolation.

Throughout the night, Chinese forces attacked from every direction. The pressure was constant and unrelenting. The 57th Field Artillery's 40 mm guns played a critical role in preventing a collapse of the line, cutting down attackers advancing around nearby structures. The effects of the fire were visible by the light of burning buildings as wave after wave pressed closer to the perimeter.

The fighting continued without pause until morning. Although no decisive breakthrough occurred, the cost was severe. Company K and Company L suffered such heavy casualties that the two units were forced to physically merge into a single defensive position. Ammunition dwindled, exhaustion set in, and the perimeter grew steadily thinner.

Recognizing the exposed position of the 1/32 Battalion to the north, Colonel MacLean ordered a withdrawal south to the 3/31 perimeter in the early morning hours of November 29, at approximately 0200 hours. He directed that all weapons and wounded be brought along. Vehicles were disabled, wounded were loaded into trucks, and the movement was set to begin at 0500 hours when daylight would come and Marine aircraft could support the withdrawal.

Death of Colonel MacLean: Dawn, November 29

At first light, the 1/32 Battalion began its fighting withdrawal to the 3/31 perimeter. The movement took place under intense enemy fire. When the lead elements reached the 3/31 perimeter, they found it still under heavy attack. Lieutenant Colonel Faith led a party of men that successfully drove the CCF off the bridge and cleared the area in front of Company K's position.

Colonel MacLean then came forward in his jeep when he spotted a column of troops approaching whom he believed was his long-overdue 2/31 Battalion. The troops were Chinese Communist Forces who had concealed themselves near the bridge.

"MacLean, believing they were American, ran toward them shouting, 'Those are my boys!' He dashed out onto the frozen reservoir, attempting to stop what he believed was friendly fire."

As MacLean advanced across the ice, the CCF soldiers suddenly unleashed a burst of gunfire, striking him multiple times. His men could only watch in horror as enemy soldiers seized the wounded colonel and dragged him into the surrounding brush. MacLean died from his injuries four days later while in Chinese captivity, on December 3, becoming the second—and ultimately the last—American regimental commander killed during the Korean War.

Given that Company K held the defensive sector directly opposite the bridge, it is plausible that my father witnessed—or at least heard—the chaotic scene in which the colonel was captured. At that moment, with the capture and subsequent death of Colonel MacLean, RCT-31's unofficial designation changed from Task Force MacLean to Task Force Faith.

Faith Takes Command and Consolidation: November 29, Daytime

Once inside the perimeter, Lieutenant Colonel Faith surveyed the carnage. Hundreds of American and CCF dead littered the ground. The 3/31 Battalion had suffered over 300 casualties. Company L had been completely destroyed as a fighting unit, with the few survivors merged into Company K. With MacLean gone, Lieutenant Colonel Faith assumed command of the regiment and worked to strengthen the perimeter, consolidating the task force into one defensive position in preparation for intensified Chinese attacks.

Marine air controller Captain Stamford, attached to RCT-31 to coordinate air support, radioed for close air support and coordinated airdrops of urgently needed supplies and ammunition. Throughout the day, Marine aircraft provided close air support against Chinese positions and conducted supply drops. However, many bundles landed outside the perimeter and fell into enemy hands, including critically needed artillery ammunition. Meanwhile, following Colonel MacLean's capture, Lieutenant Colonel Faith dispatched search parties to locate him, but all efforts proved unsuccessful.

For the men of Company K and the rest of Task Force Faith, this period was a test of endurance. The extreme cold continued to take its toll—frostbite casualties mounted, weapons continued to malfunction, and the wounded suffered terribly. Medical supplies were running critically low. Morphine had frozen solid and was useless. Plasma couldn't be administered in the freezing temperatures. Many wounded men simply died from exposure.

Captain Kitz spent his time moving among his men, checking their positions, ensuring they stayed awake (falling asleep in such cold could be fatal), and maintaining morale as best he could. By this point, Company K—now including the survivors of Company L—numbered fewer than sixty combat-effective soldiers. The men displayed remarkable courage and discipline. Despite exhaustion, casualties, and the seeming hopelessness of their situation, they maintained their positions and returned fire when Chinese forces approached.

Third Night: November 29-30

While the nights of November 27-28 and November 28-29 saw fierce, coordinated Chinese assaults against Task Force Faith and Marine positions on the western side of the reservoir, the night of November 29-30 was comparatively quiet. After two nights of intense assaults failed to break the American defenses, Chinese forces ceased their major offensive attacks that night, focusing instead on consolidation and preparation rather than launching another wave of coordinated assaults. The U.S. perimeter remained intact, with only sporadic small-arms fire and occasional mortar rounds reported rather than full-scale attacks.

Some Chinese units conducted minor probes against key approaches to the American perimeter, but by dawn the lines held and major action had subsided. This lull likely reflected a calculated pause by Chinese commanders, who withdrew briefly to regroup and bring forward additional reinforcements, including heavy mortars and light artillery, for the next wave. After nights of costly frontal assaults against American positions, the CCF had suffered staggering losses and needed to reorganize before the campaign's decisive, final phases. The pause was uneasy, a momentary breath before the storm that neither side could truly escape.

Although large-scale attacks were absent, the defenders faced extreme hardships. Temperatures remained near −30°F, heavy snow continued to fall, and U.S. units stayed cut off, relying on hazardous aerial resupply for critically low ammunition and food.

Reassignment and Withdrawal Orders: November 30, Daytime

On November 30, Major General David Barr, commander of the 7th Infantry Division, arrived by helicopter with grim news for Lieutenant Colonel Faith. All X Corps units, including Task Force Faith, were now under Marine operational control and were ordered to withdraw to Hagaru-ri. However, both Barr and Marine General Oliver P. Smith agreed that the Marines were in no position to send relief forces to aid the soldiers on the east side of the reservoir. While the Marines would continue to furnish air support, Task Force Faith would have to fight its way back on their own—a daunting prospect compounded by the nearly 600 wounded they were carrying.

Fourth Night: November 30-December 1

On the evening of November 30, around 2000 hours, Chinese Communist Forces launched their fourth successive night assault. This attack was far more powerful and sustained than previous nights, reinforced by heavy mortar and light artillery fire that rained down on the perimeter throughout the long, bitter night. Wave after wave of Chinese troops pressed against Task Force Faith's positions. Only a few managed to penetrate the defenses, and those were quickly cut down. Despite inflicting severe losses on the enemy, Task Force Faith suffered heavy casualties, leading commanders to realize another major assault could not be endured.

From Company K's perspective, the night was unrelenting. The attack began early and pressed on without pause. Chinese forces unleashed a torrent of fire—120 mm mortars supported by a mix of large, medium, and light calibers—alongside relentless small-arms and machine-gun fire. The U.S. artillery battery was nearly out of ammunition, and the rifle companies themselves were dangerously low, forcing soldiers to ration every round under extreme pressure.

Unlike previous nights when Chinese forces withdrew at dawn to avoid Marine air attacks, the enemy did not pull back this time. Chinese forces remained entrenched on the low ground near the perimeter, maintaining constant pressure throughout the morning. The sustained bombardment overwhelmed the aid station, leaving a flood of wounded men in desperate need of care. For Company K and the other units, this night marked the peak of intensity before the breakout, a test of endurance, courage, and cohesion under extreme winter conditions.

The Decision to Break Out

Following the fourth night of sustained enemy attacks, Lieutenant Colonel Faith recognized that Task Force Faith could not survive in its current position. Ammunition was critically low. Casualties mounted by the hour. Medical supplies were exhausted. The cold continued to disable men and equipment alike. No relief force was coming—the Marines on the western shore were fighting their own desperate battle and could offer no assistance.

Faith made the fateful decision to attempt a breakout to the south, fighting through the Chinese encirclement to reach Hagaru-ri, approximately twelve miles away. This was the only remaining option. Staying in place meant slow destruction. Attempting to break out to the north, toward the Chinese border, was impossible. The only hope lay in fighting south along the single road to Hagaru-ri.

Preparation for Breakout

Task Force Faith prepared for the breakout attempt. All remaining supplies were loaded onto vehicles. The wounded—by now numbering in the hundreds—were placed in trucks. Units were reorganized into march columns. Captain Kitz consolidated Company K's remaining effectives and prepared them for what everyone knew would be a desperate fight.

The plan called for a column of vehicles carrying the wounded to proceed down the road, with infantry units providing security on the flanks. Marine and Navy aircraft would provide close air support, attacking Chinese positions along the route. The artillery batteries and Heavy Mortar Company were ordered to fire all remaining ammunition before the breakout, then destroy their weapons to prevent capture. It was a sound tactical plan under the circumstances, but it depended on maintaining unit cohesion and momentum during the withdrawal—qualities that would prove impossible to sustain once the column came under fire.

The Breakout Attempt: December 1, 1950

By December 1, 1950, American forces were on the brink of collapse. Ammunition stocks were critically low, and more than half of the troops were either killed or wounded, including a disproportionate number of senior leaders. Lieutenant Colonel Faith had already made the decision that a desperate southward breakout toward the Marine lines at Hagaru-ri was the only option. At 1100 hours, the formal order arrived from the commander of the 1st Marine Division. Faith gathered his remaining officers and set a departure time of 1300 hours. Only the bare essentials were kept: enough vehicles to evacuate roughly 600 wounded soldiers and minimal personal gear. All other materiel—including trucks, radios, and artillery howitzers—was deliberately destroyed on site after firing their final rounds, to prevent it from falling into enemy hands.

Column Formation and Early Movement

The breakout column was led by the 1/32 Battalion, now commanded by Major Crosby P. Miller. Behind it followed the 57th Field Artillery Battalion, the Heavy Mortar Company, and the 3/31 Battalion—which included Company K—bringing up the rear as the rear guard. This was a particularly dangerous assignment, as rear guard units typically bear the brunt of pursuing enemy attacks. At 1300 hours, the convoy departed, spearheaded by a twin-mounted 40 mm gun vehicle.

The convoy—carrying the wounded soldiers crammed into just twenty-two trucks—trudged south along the snow-slick gravel road that clung to the reservoir's edge. Enemy fire swept the surrounding hills, forcing the men to scramble for any bit of cover they could find. In his after-action report, Captain Kitz, commander of Company K, noted that maintaining any coherent formation was virtually impossible; the troops were compelled to huddle behind the trucks for protection and, whenever the fire intensified, to dash toward the reservoir's shoreline for additional shelter. My father, as one of Company K's riflemen in the rear guard, would have experienced the confusion, fear, and relentless pressure Kitz recounted.

Lieutenant Colonel Faith kept the breakout column tightly packed, limiting the convoy to those twenty-two vehicles to transport the wounded. Marine F4U Corsairs and Navy F7F twin-engine fighters delivered vital close-air support, strafing and bombing Chinese positions as the heavily burdened American column inched southward along the gravel road on the eastern shore of the Chosin Reservoir.

Friendly Fire Incident

Shortly after the column had moved out, fire erupted from concealed Chinese positions, tearing into the lead troops. In an effort to protect the convoy, four Marine pilots misjudged their napalm runs, striking the Americans instead of the enemy. Flames consumed several soldiers, killing them instantly and throwing the forward companies into chaos as they scrambled blindly, fleeing the very air support meant to protect them.

The accidental napalm strike dealt a devastating blow to morale across the task force. After briefly rallying his men, Lieutenant Colonel Faith pressed the column forward, moving past sporadic Chinese fire teams. Enemy positions pressed as close as 50-75 yards, making organized withdrawal nearly impossible. The 1/32 Battalion, tasked with leading the column, was repeatedly targeted. Roadblocks at multiple points forced the troops to move onto the ice of the reservoir, creating new hazards: sections of ice gave way under the weight of men and vehicles, plunging soldiers into freezing water. Some managed to climb out, while others did not. Captain Kitz himself fell through but survived. These trials highlighted the extreme peril of the breakout, as the troops battled both the enemy and the unforgiving terrain simultaneously.

As the convoy pressed forward, small groups of company officers—including Kitz and several artillery officers—rallied stragglers and spearheaded assaults on three key roadblocks along the route. Their decisive actions drove the remainder of the column onward and allowed the vehicles to resume movement, offering the only chance to keep the breakout alive.

The Assault on Hill 1221

By 1700 hours, all ammunition for the artillery guns was exhausted. The task force now had only small arms, a few anti-aircraft weapons, and Marine air support—the artillery that had been critical to their defense was gone. The battered column ground to a halt as it approached Hill 1221, a commanding height that dominated the gravel road below. One battalion from the Chinese 242nd Regiment had established a strong defensive position on the hill and a roadblock at its base, blocking Faith's retreat. Well-established roadblocks and a damaged bridge north of the crest made any further advance impossible without first clearing the position.

Chinese small-arms and machine-gun fire raked the road from the heights, destroying vehicles and pinning down troops in the open. Casualties among officers and noncommissioned leaders had already begun to fracture unit cohesion, and the convoy risked complete destruction if it remained trapped beneath the hill.

Recognizing that his only hope of surviving was a direct attack on Hill 1221, Faith gathered his men together, even the wounded who could hold a rifle, and ordered them to take the hill. In a desperate assault, several units attacked Hill 1221 trying to clear it. Despite exhaustion, brutal cold, and mounting losses under intense fire, these troops succeeded in driving Chinese forces from portions of the hill late in the afternoon. During the assault, Faith was wounded when a grenade exploded near him, but he continued to lead his men forward.

The assault came at a severe cost. Most critically, many men who rushed up the hill and helped clear Chinese positions did not return to the column. Instead, disoriented and scattered, they continued over the hill's far side and descended independently, heading south across the frozen Chosin Reservoir toward Marine positions at Hagaru-ri. This mass departure stripped the column of desperately needed combat strength at the very moment when cohesion was most critical. The partial seizure of Hill 1221 enabled what remained of the column to break through the immediate roadblock and resume the march south as darkness fell, but the force was now dangerously weakened and increasingly fragmented.

The Final Collapse

With Marine air cover withdrawn for the night, the column crept forward in darkness. The convoy navigated past the wreckage of charred tanks from the 31st Tank Company's action on November 30, half-buried in the snow and narrowing the passage. As night closed in, Chinese infantry attacks grew increasingly bold. The unit, already fragmented from the Hill 1221 assault and battered by mounting casualties at every level of command, teetered on the brink of collapse.

During the fighting near Hill 1221, Lieutenant Colonel Faith was mortally wounded when a grenade exploded near him. His men placed him in the cab of a truck, but his condition was grave. The column pushed forward desperately, but Faith would not survive. He died from his wounds as the convoy struggled toward Hudong-ni. With Faith's death and other senior officers killed, command disintegrated completely. No one assumed overall command in the chaos.

The Chinese renewed their attacks with devastating effect. The remnants of the column never reached Hudong-ni itself—the convoy ground to a halt short of the town. With every attack, more trucks had to be abandoned. Each attack was as disheartening as it was deadly. The few remaining able-bodied soldiers could no longer maintain cohesion. The breakout attempt had failed. Individual soldiers and small groups scattered, many fleeing across the frozen reservoir toward the west, others moving along the shoreline toward Hagaru-ri. The organized fighting strength of Task Force Faith had ceased to exist.

Lieutenant Colonel Anderson, who had commanded the rear CP at Hudong-ni before withdrawing to Hagaru-ri on November 30, tracked the breakout convoy's progress through artillery radio relay and liaison aircraft observation. He later reported that the convoy 'were able to work their trucks out just about as far as the school house, at which place they were definitely stopped. Enemy counter-measures broke them up into small groups. They were running short and, in some cases, completely out of ammunition.' Beginning on the night of December 1 and continuing through December 2, small groups and individuals began infiltrating into Hagaru-ri. As Anderson noted, 'They did not come in tactical groupings or sizeable units. They came in more as individuals. By far the largest number of people that came in early were the ROK enlisted men.' Practically all survivors arrived without their equipment—a detail that would later be used unjustly to brand them as cowards.

Reference: Lieutenant Colonel Anderson, Report on Operations, 31st Infantry, undated interview transcript [circa December 1950-March 1951], National Archives.

The Destruction of Company K and Task Force Faith

For Company K, the final hours are difficult to reconstruct with precision. Captain Kitz's after-action report ends with the final destruction of the convoy where he says he and others took off over the ice for Hagaru-ri. The subsequent chaos made detailed record-keeping impossible. What is known is that Company K, like the rest of Task Force Faith, effectively ceased to exist as an organized unit during the fighting around Hill 1221 and the final collapse. Captain Kitz and those with him escaped across the ice of the frozen reservoir, reaching the Marine perimeter at Hagaru-ri around midnight.

Of the approximately 3,200 men of Task Force Faith who had defended the perimeter (including 2,500 Americans and 700 ROK soldiers), the breakout column on December 1 consisted of roughly 600 wounded packed into trucks and perhaps 500 able-bodied troops who could still fight. Only about 385 able-bodied survivors ultimately reached Marine positions at Hagaru-ri. Some escaped across the ice of the frozen reservoir. Others continued south along the road, fighting as individuals or in small groups. A few vehicles carrying wounded managed to break through. But for most of Task Force Faith, the breakout ended in death, capture, or desperate individual escapes across the frozen mountains.

At the base of Hill 1221, and stretching nearly two miles to the north, lie the remains of approximately 1,000 American and South Korean soldiers who perished there in late November and early December 1950. On December 2, the 31st Tank Company undertook a perilous patrol back toward the abandoned positions to help evacuate wounded survivors from the 3/31 and 1/32 Battalions. Under sustained small-arms and anti-tank fire from Chinese positions, the tanks succeeded in recovering roughly twenty wounded men who had been left behind during the chaotic breakout. This mission marked one of the final attempts to rescue survivors from the eastern shore of the reservoir. The recovery of those who fell on the battlefield would continue, in some cases, for many years afterward.

My Father's Survival

Exactly how my father survived the destruction of Company K and Task Force Faith remains largely unknown—he rarely spoke of these events in detail. From the few fragments he shared, we know that during one of the night attacks his position was overrun by Chinese forces. He survived by feigning death as enemy soldiers moved through the area, probing bodies with their rifles. This moment of terror—lying motionless in the snow as enemy soldiers checked whether he was alive—must have been one of the most harrowing experiences of the entire battle. He never talked about what happened during the breakout.

During the final breakout and collapse, my father must have made his way south toward Hagaru-ri. If he remained with Captain Kitz and the other Company K survivors during the final chaos, he would have escaped across the ice of the frozen reservoir, reaching the Marine perimeter around midnight on December 1. If separated from Kitz's group, he may have traveled along the shoreline with other scattered survivors. The most likely scenario is that he crossed the ice with Kitz. Whether he traveled alone or with other survivors are details lost to history. What matters is that he survived when so many others did not.

The Final Toll

Of the approximately 3,200 men of Task Force Faith (including 2,500 Americans and 700 ROK soldiers), only about 1,000 reached Hagaru-ri. Of these, approximately 385 were able-bodied and capable of continued military service, while the remainder were wounded, frostbitten, or so exhausted and traumatized as to require evacuation and extended recovery. My father was among the 385 able-bodied survivors—a testament to both his physical resilience and his will to survive.

The casualties speak to the magnitude of the disaster: approximately 2,200 men killed, missing, or captured. These numbers represent one of the worst regimental-level disasters in U.S. Army history. The destruction was so complete that the 31st Infantry Regiment, which had served with distinction in World War II, was temporarily struck from the active Army rolls following the battle. The regiment was reconstituted just days later and attached to the 1st Marine Division for the fighting withdrawal south to the port city of Hungnam, where X Corps would be evacuated by sea.

Aftermath and Legacy

Reorganization at Hagaru-ri: December 2-7, 1950

The survivors who reached Hagaru-ri on the night of December 1 and throughout December 2 faced immediate reorganization. The approximately 385 able-bodied survivors from Task Force Faith, along with other 7th Infantry Division elements, were rapidly formed into a provisional regiment under the reconstituted 31st Infantry Regimental Headquarters. This force was organized into two battalions, with the 3rd Battalion commanded by Major Witte (the former S-3 of the 31st Infantry).

My father's Company K was reorganized under Captain Kitz, who had led the company through the five-day ordeal east of the reservoir. The new K Company was formed by consolidating the survivors of the original K Company with remnants of M Company and elements of the 31st Infantry Headquarters. Each rifle company numbered approximately 90 men—roughly half American and half Republic of Korea soldiers. The companies had no crew-served weapons. Personnel were armed only with pistols, M-1 rifles, carbines, and an assortment of captured enemy weapons. All fire support had to come through Marine channels, as the provisional regiment possessed no supporting weapons of its own.

From December 2 through December 7, the provisional 31st Regimental Combat Team defended an assigned sector of the Hagaru-ri perimeter against constant Chinese Communist Forces attacks. The Marines on the western side of the reservoir had fought their way into Hagaru-ri and were preparing for a coordinated withdrawal south. During these five days, my father and his fellow soldiers—exhausted, frostbitten, and still reeling from the trauma of the previous week—held their positions and repulsed repeated enemy assaults. They had survived the destruction of Task Force Faith only to face another week of continuous combat in sub-zero temperatures.

The reorganization brought together a diverse mix of survivors. Captain Rasula, who commanded I Company in the provisional 3rd Battalion, noted that his entire company consisted of artillerymen from the 57th Field Artillery Battalion, with three artillery officers serving as platoon leaders—men trained as gunners now serving as riflemen. Captain Kitz commanded K Company, while Lieutenant Boyer led L Company. The provisional regiment's three field kitchens operated around the clock, with each kitchen serving over 1,000 men per meal as Marines from the western side of the reservoir joined the Army survivors in the defensive perimeter.

Reference: Captain George A. Rasula, Summary of Operations in the Chosin Reservoir Area, undated personal account, National Archives.

The Fighting Withdrawal to Hungnam: December 7-11, 1950

On December 7, the 31st Infantry Regimental Combat Team was reconstituted as a provisional battalion and attached to the 7th Marine Regiment. The 31st Tank Company was attached to the 5th Marine Regiment. The provisional battalion was assigned the left flank of the lead unit during the attack south from Hagaru-ri toward Koto-ri and ultimately Hungnam. The Army soldiers would screen the eastern approaches, securing high ground up to 1,000 yards from the main supply route as the Marine column fought its way south.

That evening, approximately four miles south of Hagaru-ri, the column encountered a strong Chinese roadblock. The 1st Battalion of the provisional force was committed to the fight and overran the heavily fortified enemy positions. During the night of December 7-8, the provisional battalion received two strong enemy attacks, both of which were repulsed. The fighting was intense enough that the Task Force Air Control Party trucks and the 31st Infantry Regimental Command Post trucks were destroyed in these attacks. My father, serving in K Company on the exposed left flank, would have been directly engaged in this fighting.

During the night fighting south of Hagaru-ri, Chinese forces launched a counterattack from the left flank behind American positions, overrunning part of the column. Lieutenant Colonel Anderson's command post truck and the Tactical Air Control Party vehicle were destroyed, and all CP personnel except Anderson himself were hit. The attack demonstrated how vulnerable the column remained to infiltration tactics even during organized withdrawal.

The column reached Koto-ri on the morning of December 8. The provisional 31st Battalion was ordered to attack two hills northwest of Koto-ri to keep the main supply route open against enemy attacks. The hills were secured against light opposition. Considerable reorganization was required because of the number of casualties sustained during the fighting from Hagaru-ri to Koto-ri. By this point, only two companies could be assembled to continue the movement, and the regimental staff had lost all personnel.

On December 9, the attack south resumed. The first two objectives were secured against light enemy opposition, but then an intense snowstorm struck. Captain Rasula, commanding I Company, later recalled that visibility dropped to twenty-five yards and control became extremely difficult. Temperatures plummeted to between -10°F and -20°F. The soldiers dug into frozen ground on exposed hilltops and endured a night of brutal cold with inadequate winter gear. According to Rasula, the provisional battalion 'lost more men due to cold that night than we lost all the way from Hagaru-ri.' The casualties from exposure during the blizzard of December 9-10 exceeded the combat losses sustained during the entire fighting withdrawal from the reservoir.

Reference: Captain George A. Rasula, Summary of Operations in the Chosin Reservoir Area, undated personal account, National Archives.

The extreme cold proved as deadly as enemy fire. Lieutenant Colonel Anderson, commanding the provisional battalion, observed that by the final days at Koto-ri he had to implement a 50% rotation system—half the men in defensive positions while half were in warming areas where they could dry their socks and innersoles and restore feeling to their bodies. Radio communications broke down completely during the worst cold; of eight radios, only two functioned. As Anderson later reflected, 'in that type of country and in those types of temperatures, I question the feasibility, considering our mechanized type of Army of large scale operations, being successful.'

On the morning of December 10, the provisional battalion continued the attack south from Koto-ri. Eventually, the soldiers reached friendly positions where they were loaded onto trucks and transported to Hungnam. There, individuals were directed back to their parent units for evacuation or reassignment.

The Continuing Toll

The retreat from Chosin was brutal. On December 1, the 3rd Battalion, 31st Infantry—including K Company—numbered roughly 1,000 men as they reached Hagaru-ri. Many were wounded or suffering from severe frostbite and were evacuated by air to Japan for treatment. The survivors who remained endured continuous enemy attacks, sub-zero temperatures, and extreme exhaustion as they marched south to Hungnam between December 1 and 11. According to the official 3rd Battalion Command Report, by the time the provisional battalion completed its movement into Hungnam on December 11, only one officer and 47 enlisted men from the 3rd Battalion had made it all the way through. The attrition from combat, wounds, frostbite, and exhaustion was devastating.

Reference: 31st Infantry Regimental Combat Team, Command Report: 1–11 December 1950, 11 March 1950, National Archives.

My father survived. Whether he was among the 47 men who fought continuously for ten more days after escaping the Chosin Reservoir, or was evacuated earlier due to wounds or frostbite, remains unknown. That he never mentioned being wounded or frostbitten lends credence to the possibility that he was one of those 47 who endured the full retreat. We will likely never know for certain. What is certain is that he continued to fight with the remnants of the 7th Infantry Division, which was subsequently reconstituted and returned to combat in early 1951, participating in the push back to the 38th parallel, where the war would ultimately end with an armistice. My father continued to serve in the conflict until his honorable discharge from active duty in August 1951, having endured and survived one of the most brutal campaigns in American military history.

Legacy and Historical Truth

Initial Misrepresentation: 1950-1970s

For decades after Chosin, RCT-31's extraordinary performance was overshadowed by misunderstanding and misrepresentation. Military leaders and historians misinterpreted the condition of arriving survivors as proof of cowardice and collapse. Even General O.P. Smith, commander of the 1st Marine Division, dismissed them as "Army jokers," while a Navy chaplain openly labeled them cowards. The military's Stars and Stripes publication offered no kinder assessment.

Critics focused particularly on the weaponless condition of many RCT-31 survivors, viewing this as evidence of panic and abandonment of duty. This judgment ignored the brutal reality of RCT-31's situation. Artillery and mortars had been destroyed by direct order before the breakout to prevent capture. Small arms ammunition was exhausted during hours of continuous fighting against overwhelming enemy forces. Soldiers who survived the destruction of their positions were forced to escape on foot—more than half carrying wounds—across frozen terrain where retaining empty weapons served no tactical purpose. Yet beneath these harsh judgments lay a story of near-impossible endurance—a regiment battered by freezing cold, relentless enemy attacks, and staggering losses, fighting not for glory but simply to survive.

Official Secrecy and Suppression

Following the battle, all records and personal statements regarding RCT-31's actions were classified as "Secret," remaining sealed for fifty years in an effort to conceal the full extent of the Chosin disaster. Key Army documents, especially those detailing the rapid and fatal redeployment of RCT-31, were withheld from public scrutiny for decades.

The official suppression was compounded by personal silence. Survivors returned home to a society eager to move past war, often finding little understanding or acknowledgment of their ordeal. Many veterans bore physical and emotional scars in isolation, their stories largely unspoken. The Korean War became known as "the forgotten war," and the heroism and suffering at Chosin remained largely unrecognized outside military circles.

The Truth Emerges

As classified documents were gradually declassified and released, the full story began to emerge. The release of Chinese military documents and scholarly research—particularly Army historian Roy Appleman's comprehensive work on the Korean War—reshaped the narrative. Chinese records confirmed that RCT-31 was vastly outnumbered and under-resourced, and that its sustained resistance significantly diverted enemy pressure from the Marines, enabling a more orderly withdrawal from the western side of the reservoir.

Only after the release of thousands of pages of Marine Corps records, Army after-action reports, and personal testimonies did the public begin to grasp the scale of the ordeal and the extraordinary resilience shown by the soldiers at Chosin. The historical reassessment revealed that RCT-31 had not collapsed in cowardice but had fought a desperate five-day defensive action against impossible odds, inflicting substantial casualties on Chinese forces while protecting the Marine Division's exposed flank.

Today, the battle is recognized as one of the most harrowing episodes in Korean War history. The sacrifice of RCT-31 stands as a testament to individual courage and unit cohesion in the face of systemic challenges often beyond their control, embodying the finest traditions of American military service under the most adverse conditions imaginable.

Mission Accomplishment and Strategic Value

Historical reassessment has vindicated RCT-31's performance. Despite the task force's destruction, it accomplished the essential aspects of its mission. For five days, RCT-31 successfully guarded the right flank of the 1st Marine Division, absorbing attacks from the Chinese 80th and 81st Divisions that otherwise would have fallen on Marine positions.

Historians now recognize that RCT-31's actions helped prevent the Chinese forces from capturing Hagaru-ri, which at the time was critically undermanned and vulnerable. As the vital Marine base and airstrip, Hagaru-ri needed time for Marine units scattered along the reservoir to concentrate sufficient defensive forces. The presence of RCT-31 east of the reservoir, drawing enemy attention and combat power, provided that critical time. Without RCT-31's sacrifice, the complete encirclement and potential annihilation of the entire 1st Marine Division might have been unavoidable. The task force's fierce resistance inflicted heavy losses on attacking Chinese divisions, degrading their combat effectiveness for subsequent operations.

Closing Thoughts

The Horror of War

War is hell, and what my father endured at Chosin was horrific and terrifying beyond imagination. The Korean War, often called the "Forgotten War," was anything but forgettable for the men who fought in its brutal conditions. My father's experience at the Chosin Reservoir represents one of the most harrowing chapters in American military history, where temperatures plummeted to minus 35 degrees Fahrenheit and Chinese forces surrounded and attacked our troops in overwhelming numbers.

The Burden of Survival

As one of only 385 able-bodied survivors from Task Force Faith, my father witnessed firsthand the devastating toll of modern warfare. The psychological weight of survival, knowing that so many others didn't make it home, must have been enormous. Survivor's guilt—a common and deeply painful experience among combat veterans—likely haunted him long after the guns fell silent.

While the term PTSD wasn't formally recognized until 1980—in his time it was called "combat fatigue" or "shell shock"—my father, like so many others who endured the crucible of Chosin, was deeply traumatized by the ordeal. The invisible wounds of war—anxiety, nightmares, and the relentless replaying of traumatic memories—accompanied him long after he returned home. His survival was a testament to his resilience, but it came at a profound emotional cost that shaped the rest of his life.

The Injustice and Vindication

For decades after the war, the Army units that fought at Chosin faced an additional burden beyond their physical and psychological wounds: they were unjustly branded as cowards who had fled in the face of the enemy. This cruel mischaracterization added insult to the grievous injuries these brave men had already endured. The truth about their heroic stand against overwhelming odds, their strategic fighting withdrawal that protected the Marine Division's flank, and their crucial role in enabling the successful withdrawal of X Corps from Hungnam was buried beneath layers of military politics and incomplete historical accounts.

As discussed earlier, only after decades of classified documents being released and scholarly research has the full truth about the Army's role at Chosin emerged. These soldiers didn't retreat in cowardice—they conducted one of the most remarkable fighting withdrawals in military history, holding off vastly superior Chinese forces while protecting the eastern flank of the UN withdrawal. The fact that my father survived Chosin and continued to serve with distinction until his discharge speaks to the remarkable character of his generation.

The Character of a Patriot

These men faced unimaginable hardships with courage and determination, often at tremendous personal cost. They bore not only the physical and psychological wounds of battle, but also the unjust shame of being misunderstood and maligned by their own country. Yet their dedication to duty never wavered.

Among my memories of my father, one captures the depth of his patriotism and his understanding of duty. I recall his genuine satisfaction when President Truman relieved General Douglas MacArthur of his command for insubordination in April 1951. My father fully grasped the weight of MacArthur's errors—the overconfidence, the dismissal of intelligence warnings, the orders that sent men into impossible situations. Yet even MacArthur's tragic mistakes could never diminish my father's love for his country or his unshakable sense of duty.

That steadfast patriotism, I believe, was forged in the crucible of Chosin during five harrowing days when courage and commitment were tested to their absolute limits alongside his fallen and surviving comrades. It remained an essential part of who he was decades later—a powerful reminder that true devotion to one's country endures through every trial, every hardship, and every disappointment. His generation understood that serving your country meant accepting both its triumphs and its failures, its wise decisions and its tragic errors, and continuing to serve with honor regardless.

The Legacy

In reflecting on my father's experience, I am reminded that behind every military statistic and historical account stands a human being who faced fear, pain, and loss with courage. His story is not just one of survival, but of service, sacrifice, and the enduring strength of the human spirit in the face of overwhelming adversity.

The Korean War may have been called "forgotten," but my father's service—and the service of all those who fought alongside him—deserves lasting recognition. Their courage, endurance, and sacrifice helped preserve the freedoms we often take for granted today, and their example continues to teach future generations the meaning of duty, honor, and the true cost of liberty.

I have written this account not merely to honor my father's memory, but to ensure that my children and grandchildren will always know about this man whose courage and sacrifice helped shape the world they inherited. They should know that their grandfather faced the unthinkable with quiet dignity and never spoke of his own bravery. They should know what he endured, what he survived, and what it cost him.

This is your grandfather's story. This is my father's legacy.
