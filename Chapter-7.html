Life as an Expat: Adjusting to a Muslim Country

(1984-1987)

Three years in the 'magic' kingdom‚Äîa land of countless contradictions‚Äîwhere Mecca, a holy sanctuary, is reserved exclusively for Muslim worshippers

In 1984, an extraordinary opportunity redirected my life's trajectory: a chance to work for an American consulting firm in **Saudi Arabia**. This was a defining moment, one that required uprooting my family‚Äîmy wife, Pam, and one-year-old daughter, Jessica‚Äîfrom Minnesota to Riyadh. It was a daunting prospect, but the adventure, the career advancement, and the chance to live out my anthropological passion in a wholly new culture were too compelling to ignore.

Life in Riyadh was defined by its dichotomies. Professionally, I thrived, applying my research skills as a technical writer and editor for a large contract managing the development of military facilities. The work was demanding and allowed me to delve into complex organizational structures and cross-cultural communication challenges. This was a fascinating, behind-the-scenes look at how the military industrial complex operated in a foreign land.

Socially and personally, the adjustment was significant. Riyadh was a strictly conservative Muslim society. The daily call to prayer, the segregation of sexes, and the pervasive religious influence required my family to adapt to a vastly different rhythm of life. We lived on a segregated compound, which, while offering modern amenities and a sense of community with other expats, insulated us from the full experience of Saudi life. This compound existence, however, fostered lifelong friendships with people from around the globe, creating a truly international social circle.

This period marked a profound chapter in my ongoing cultural education. It was here that I learned the true meaning of **cultural relativism** and the patience required for cross-cultural work. It solidified my identity as a global citizen‚Äîsomeone who could not only observe but immerse and adapt. Leaving Saudi Arabia in 1987, after the contract ended, was a sad farewell to friends and to a unique, challenging, and highly formative experience. I returned to the U.S. not just with savings, but with a deeper professional capability and a much broader human understanding, ready for the next phase of my career at the Minnesota Department of Human Services.
